# Discussion Points
Here is a [master list of logical fallacies](https://utminers.utep.edu/omwilliamson/ENGL1311/fallacies.htm). These are the ones I want to focus on:
* Ad Hominem Argument (Personal attacks against the person making an argument instead of addressing the logic behind their argument)
* Affective Fallacy ("I feel it, so it must be true!")
* Appeal to Heaven (The belief in a god or higher power that dictates their actions.)
* Appeal to Nature ("If it's found in nature, then it must be good!")
* Appeal to Pity (Urging people to root for the "underdog" regardless of the issues at hand)
* Appeal to Authority (The idea that every tradition exists for a rational reason)
* Argument from Ignorance (You don't know whether a claim is true or false, so it must be true (or false))
* Availability Bias (Giving undue attention and importance to information that is immediately available at hand)
* The Bandwagon ("Something is common and/or a lot of people are doing it, so it must be good!")
* Brainwashing ("My enemies only believe in X because they believe propaganda!"/"Our side only has facts and logic; no propaganda.")
* Circular Reasoning ("You need experience to land a job; you need a job to land experience.")
* Confirmation Bias (People come to a conclusion first and only pay attention that reaffirms said conclusion)
* Cost Bias ("Something that costs more must be better!")
* Dog-Whistle Politics (Using a brief phrase or slogan-of-the-hour to distract from the argument)
* Goomba Fallacy (Members of a group do not think the same; they may contradict each other. Using one's claims against another is conflating the two.)

# Examples
* "100% of people who were born and breathed oxygen have eventually died. This means that oxygen is the primary cause of death in humans."
* (example of an appeal to __) "Then why should I pay attention to what you say?"

# How to Avoid Fallacies
* Evaluate the logic behind a claim
* Examine if there is contradictory evidence
* Do not place value of a claim solely on the one making the claim

Do not say "it's always been that way, so it must be true!", ask "based on past evidence, it seems to be this way; why is this?".
